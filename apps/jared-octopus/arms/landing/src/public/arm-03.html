PowerManagement.

Arm 03 — Keep the lights on. Monitors every power rail, enforces the 150W energy budget across all active arms, and throttles before things break.

03

What it does

Real-time rail monitoring

Arm 03 reads current and voltage from INA219 sensors on each power rail at 10 Hz. It maintains a rolling 5-second window of power consumption per arm and compares it against configured soft and hard limits. Soft limits trigger a warning to the head; hard limits trigger immediate throttling without waiting for head approval — latency is too high when a driver is about to thermal-shutdown.

The arm tracks not just instantaneous draw but trend. If arm 02's SDR is gradually drawing more current over an observation session (common when the USB chip heats up), arm 03 will predict the hard limit breach and pre-emptively alert the head, buying time to gracefully pause before a hard cut is needed.

Budget enforcement and throttle logic

The total energy budget is 150W for a standard 200W bench supply, leaving 50W headroom for transients. Arm 03 assigns each arm a budget slice (configurable; defaults: arm-01 40W, arm-02 12W, arm-04 5W, arm-05 10W, arm-06 3W, head 10W). When a fast slew or calibration run requests a transient burst, arm 03 evaluates whether other arms can be throttled momentarily to cover it.

Throttle commands flow through the head — arm 03 never contacts other arms directly. It reports "arm-01 needs 35W for fast slew; arm-05 can yield 20W temporarily" and the head issues the throttle directive to arm 05 and the approval to arm 01. This keeps the acyclic graph intact even under energy pressure.

Thermal watchdog

A Dallas DS18B20 1-Wire temperature sensor mounted on the A4988 stepper driver and the bench supply heatsink gives arm 03 thermal readings every 2 seconds. It maintains safe operating thresholds: warn at 60°C, throttle at 70°C, emergency shutdown at 80°C. On emergency shutdown, arm 03 issues park commands to arm 01 and stop commands to arm 02 via the head before cutting power to non-essential rails.

The system's immune system. Arm 03 is the only arm that can trigger an emergency response without waiting for the head to respond.

Status

  • State: Active
  • Sensor bus: I²C (INA219) + 1-Wire (DS18B20)
  • Sample rate: 10 Hz per rail
  • Rails monitored: 5 (12V motor, 5V servo, 5V USB, 3.3V logic, 5V SDR)
  • Total budget: 150W (200W supply, 50W headroom)
  • Energy draw: <1W (sensors only)

Hardware

The sensor stack.

INA219 current/voltage sensors

Texas Instruments INA219 — 12-bit ADC, I²C interface, measures both shunt voltage (current) and bus voltage. One per monitored rail, wired in series on the positive supply line. I²C addresses configured via A0/A1 pins (0x40–0x4F range). Maximum measurable current depends on shunt resistor: 0.1Ω gives ±3.2A range, 0.01Ω gives ±32A. Each sensor adds <0.5% measurement error at full scale.

DS18B20 temperature sensors

Dallas/Maxim DS18B20 waterproof probe. 1-Wire bus, up to 8 sensors on a single GPIO pin. 12-bit resolution = 0.0625°C. Conversion time 750ms at full resolution. Arm 03 reads all thermal sensors in parallel using the DS18B20 simultaneous convert command. Probes are mounted with thermal paste on the A4988 heatsink, bench supply exhaust, and optional SDR enclosure.

Wiring summary

  • Pi I²C → INA219 ×5: SDA, SCL, 3.3V, GND; each at unique address
  • INA219 shunt: 0.1Ω on motor rail, 0.01Ω on USB/SDR rails
  • Pi GPIO4 → DS18B20 bus: 4.7kΩ pull-up to 3.3V, up to 8 sensors
  • Emergency relay: GPIO-controlled solid-state relay on 12V motor rail
  • Soft power: GPIO signal to bench supply remote-sense input

MCP Tools

The interface.

power_status()

Return current power draw, voltage, and temperature readings for all rails and sensors. The head calls this before dispatching energy-intensive tasks.

Returns: { rails: [{name, voltage_v, current_a, power_w, headroom_w}], thermals: [{sensor, temp_c, status}], budget_used_w: float }
Latency: <15ms
throttle(arm_id: string, level: string)

Request that the named arm reduce its power draw. Level is "warn" (notify only), "soft" (reduce to 70% of allocation), or "hard" (reduce to 40%). Only the head issues this command — arm 03 recommends, head decides.

Returns: { ok: bool, arm_id, level, new_limit_w: float }
Example: throttle("arm-05", "soft")
approve_burst(arm_id: string, watts: float, duration_ms: int)

Pre-approve a transient power burst for an arm that will exceed its normal allocation. Arm 03 evaluates whether the total budget can absorb it, optionally scheduling compensating throttles on other arms, then grants or denies.

Returns: { approved: bool, available_w: float, compensated_arms: string[] }
Example: approve_burst("arm-01", 35.0, 2000)
emergency_shutdown(reason: string)

Cut non-essential rails immediately. Only called by arm 03 itself (on thermal or overcurrent detection) or the head on critical fault. Sends park/stop signals via head before cutting power. Logs reason with full rail snapshot.

Returns: { ok: bool, rails_cut: string[], timestamp_ms: int }
Note: 3.3V logic and Pi power remain on

Failure modes

What breaks.
How it recovers.

sensor dropout

INA219 stops responding on I²C

Arm 03 marks that rail as unmonitored, reduces the effective budget by the rail's maximum allocation (conservative), and alerts the head. Science continues but the head will decline energy-intensive requests until the sensor is confirmed healthy.

thermal runaway

Temperature rises faster than throttling can compensate

If temperature increases by more than 5°C in 30 seconds despite active throttling, arm 03 escalates directly to emergency shutdown without waiting for the head. The head is notified post-fact. This is the one case where the acyclic graph is bypassed — physics doesn't wait for arbitration.

budget exceeded

Total draw exceeds 150W hard limit

Arm 03 cuts the highest-draw non-critical arm first (typically arm 05 or 06), then re-evaluates. If still over budget, cuts the next. Arm 01 and arm 02 are last to be cut — losing positioning or an observation mid-session is expensive.
