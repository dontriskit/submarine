Board Gallery

Five PCBs · Concept renders · Pre-schematic

These are not fab files. They are concept renders — proportional outlines of the five custom PCBs in the 5-DOM proof-of-concept, dimensioned from the DESIGN.md commitments and laid out by function. Real schematics, copper, and Gerber outputs come after a human (Adam) sits down with KiCad. This page is what the boards look like in the head before any of that happens.

Read across in data-flow order: photons enter at board I, digital events leave through II, both ride power conditioned by III, climb the tether to the surface IV, and are cross-checked on the bench with the light source V.

⁂ System Block · DOM → Surface → Cloud ⁂

№ I

SiPM Analog Front-End

100 × 100 mm · 4-layer · ×5 + 2 spares

Stack-up
4-layer · signal / GND / power / signal
Critical net
HV +30 V bus with per-channel 1 kΩ + 100 nF decoupling
Channels
16 × (SiPM → TIA → diff driver)
Materials
~€340 / board · ~€2,430 for run + spares

The defining face of the DOM. Sixteen MicroFJ-30035 SiPMs sit in a 4×4 grid behind the optical window of the housing; each one feeds its own LMH6629 transimpedance amp directly to its left or right, with the THS4541 diff drivers running along the bottom edge and the analog rails entering through the right-hand connector zone.

The HV boost lives at the top-left, deliberately far from the analog input nets. A muted band across the top of the array represents the decoupling-cap field and the guard rings around the bias bus — what you draw last in KiCad and what makes or breaks the noise floor.

№ II

DOM Digital Mainboard

100 × 100 mm · 4–6 layer · ×5 + 2 spares

FPGA
Sipeed Tang Primer 25K · ECP5
MCU
STM32H743 (WeAct module)
ADC path
2 × AD9249 · 16 ch · 65 MSPS · LVDS
Timing
10 MHz TCXO + soft-TDC ~150 ps
Materials
~€180 / board · ~€1,310 for run + spares

The digital brain. Analog enters from board I through the left-edge connector, splits across two AD9249 8-channel ADCs, and crosses the board as LVDS pairs into the ECP5 FPGA. The TCXO sits right next to the FPGA's PLL pin — the shortest critical net on the whole DOM.

The STM32 module takes events from the FPGA over SPI and pushes them out through the LAN8720 PHY, the magnetics, and a Subconn 8-pin bulkhead on the right edge. PPS arrives on that same connector and is buffered by a DS90LV019 LVDS receiver. No GPS on this board — that's centralised at the surface.

№ III

Power Conditioning

80 × 60 mm · 4-layer · ×5 + 2 spares

Input
+48 V from tether (pins 1–2)
Rails out
+12 V_a · +5 V_a · +5 V_d · +3.3 V_d · +1.8 V_a
Topology
1 × isolated brick + 3 × buck + 1 × low-noise LDO
Budget
~8.4 W per DOM · 20 % margin → 10 W
Materials
~€70 / board · ~€470 for run + spares

The cheapest, dumbest, most load-bearing board of the five. The isolated brick is physically the largest single component on the DOM — it breaks the ground loop between surface and detector and dissipates ~1 W of its own. Thermally bonded to the housing wall.

POL bucks line up along the digital edge, the LT3045 LDO produces the quiet +1.8 V_a for the ADCs, and an LC post-filter cleans the analog rails before they cross to boards I and II. The HV monitor comparator listens to board I's bias bus and trips a fault line if the SiPMs go dark.

№ IV

Surface Aggregator

100 × 80 mm · 4-layer · ×1 + 1 spare

Lives in
Pelican 1450-class case on RIB / buoy
Power
12 V LiFePO4 → boost to 48 V → 5 tethers
Timing master
uBlox NEO-M9N + NB3N551 1:5 LVDS fan-out
External modules
Pi 5 · GbE switch · LTE modem · battery
Materials
~€270 / PCB + ~€650 external · ~€900 / built

The bridge between five DOMs and the cloud. Five identical tether output blocks run down the left edge — each one a TPS25940 eFuse, TVS array, common-mode choke, and a Subconn bulkhead pointing out the side of the case. A short on any one tether trips its own eFuse without disturbing the others.

The single GPS module sits top-right with its SMA going up to the mast; its 1 PPS fans out through the NB3N551 to all five tether pairs with under 50 ps skew. The board is intentionally not a full system — Pi 5, GbE switch, LTE modem, and battery are all separate modules wired in via short patches.

№ V

LED Calibration Pulser

50 × 30 mm · 2-layer · ×1 + 1 spare

Drive
LMG1020 GaN, sub-2 ns rise time
LED
Cree XP-E2 or OSRAM LB W5SM · 450 nm
Trigger
Tang Nano 9K → LVDS pigtail → DOM
Attenuation
External 3-position ND filter wheel
Materials
~€25 / PCB + ~€115 optics · ~€140 / built

The smallest and cheapest board, and the one that determines whether the other four are trustworthy. A Tang Nano 9K dominates the silk: it owns USB control, schedules pulses, and emits the LVDS trigger that the DOM under test stamps with its own clock — so the pulser's time is set by the receiver, not the source.

The LMG1020 GaN driver and the 450 nm LED sit at the right edge with a 5 Ω series resistor; downstream of the board, a ground-glass diffuser and a 3-position ND filter wheel land photons on the DOM's optical window across four decades of intensity. Single-PE histograms from this rig should match cosmic-muon-derived gain to 5 %.
